Event Storming: A Visual Guide

A comprehensive, professional overview of the three levels of Event Storming and their key color-coded notations.

What is Event Storming?

Event Storming is a collaborative, rapid modeling technique used to explore complex business domains. It helps diverse teams‚Äîfrom developers to domain experts‚Äîcreate a shared understanding of a system by identifying and mapping out a sequence of Domain Events (things that happened). The visual, sticky-note-based process scales from a broad strategic overview to detailed software design.

This initial level is for exploring the business domain at a high level. The goal is to maximize learning and identify the major events, actors, and external systems. It‚Äôs perfect for getting a strategic overview and spotting potential hotspots.

Domain Events

A significant event that happened relevant to the business (e.g., "Order Placed").

Actors

A person or group who performs actions (e.g., "Customer").

External Systems

External services or "black boxes" we interact with (e.g., "Payment Gateway").

Hotspots

Problems, risks, or areas that require further discussion‚Äîkeep them visible until the end.

Opportunities

Ideas for improvements or solutions already in mind‚Äîcapture them for follow-up at the end.

2. Process Modeling Event Storming ‚öôÔ∏è

Building on the Big Picture, this level details a specific process from start to finish. It introduces new notations to model the precise, repeatable pattern: Event ‚Üí Policy ‚Üí Command ‚Üí System ‚Üí Event.

Practice tip: walk the flow backwards to confirm every event has a valid predecessor and to uncover missing steps.

Domain Events

A fact that may trigger policies or follow-up actions.

Commands

An explicit intent to change the state of the system, written as verb + noun (e.g., "Issue Invoice").

Policies

Automated business rules that act as the "glue" between an Event and a Command.

Read Models

Information needed to make or automate a decision‚ÄîUI views, reports, lookup tables, or precomputed data projections.

Systems

A software service that accepts commands and emits events‚Äîtreat as a black box and capture its contract/failure modes; internal ones often evolve into aggregates you own.

Hotspots

Alternative flows, exceptions, or questions that need attention.

Actors

Human intervention required within a process, such as a user approving a command.

3. Bounded Context Discovery üß≠

After your process slice is understood, zoom out to identify natural boundaries. Look for shifts in language, ownership, policies, and specialised knowledge‚Äîeach is a signal that different parts of the story should live in separate bounded contexts.

Discovery Checklist

  • ‚Ä¢Language shifts (different terms for the same concept)
  • ‚Ä¢Ownership or responsibility changes between teams
  • ‚Ä¢Policies or business rules that diverge
  • ‚Ä¢Knowledge that only one group possesses

Make It Visible

  • ‚Ä¢Draw dotted boundaries around clusters on the wall
  • ‚Ä¢Name each context with the ubiquitous language it owns
  • ‚Ä¢Add translation stickies at context touchpoints
  • ‚Ä¢If debate lasts more than ~3 minutes, mark a hotspot ‚ÄúBoundary TBD‚Äù and move on

4. Software Design Event Storming üíª

This final, most detailed level extends Process Modeling to design the software architecture. The focus shifts to identifying Aggregates‚Äîthe core components you will build‚Äîand their interactions. Internal pink systems you identified earlier usually turn into aggregates you own, while true external systems remain pink.

Aggregates

A single yellow sticky you design and own (not a container); keep related commands on one side and emitted events on the other.

Domain Events

Events emitted by an Aggregate in response to a command.

Commands

Actions that are accepted by a specific Aggregate.

Read Models

Decision-support information derived from events or authoritative sources; keep command-side data out.

Policies

The reactive logic that triggers commands for other Aggregates.

External Systems

External services you integrate with; keep them as black boxes and document contracts/failure modes.

Hotspots

Problems that need further architectural or design decisions.

Actors

The people initiating commands to a specific Aggregate.
